% ======================== ABSTRACT ========================
\begin{abstract}
We construct and evaluate a dynamic equity portfolio drawn from ten diversified
constituents of the French CAC~40 index, using one-minute OHLC data over the period
September~2022 -- February~2023 (120 trading days). Portfolio weights are rebalanced
daily via a Markowitz mean--variance optimisation in which expected returns are proxied
by an AR(1) forecast based on the lag-1 autocorrelation of each stock's daily returns.
All pairwise correlations are positive (average $\approx 0.29$), reflecting common
European equity exposure, while lag-1 autocorrelations lie in $[-0.168,\, 0.123]$.
Because the optimisation is unconstrained, these weak signals produce extreme leverage
(time-averaged absolute weights summing to $\approx 22$), which causes catastrophic
realised performance: the portfolio value ends at $-1271.82$ (base~100) versus $+122.89$
for the equal-weight benchmark. This result powerfully illustrates the dangers of
combining unconstrained Markowitz optimisation with low-signal return forecasts and
full-sample covariance estimation.
\end{abstract}

% ======================== 1. INTRODUCTION ========================
\section{Introduction}

The assessment of portfolio risk and performance is a central problem in quantitative
finance. The Markowitz (1952) mean--variance framework provides a tractable closed-form
solution: given a vector of expected returns and a covariance matrix, the optimal weight
vector that minimises variance subject to a target return (or, under the unit-sum
constraint used here, that maximises the expected return for a given variance) is
uniquely determined.

In this project we instantiate a simplified Markowitz portfolio on the CAC~40 French
blue-chip index, restricted to ten liquid and sector-diversified constituents over
120 trading days (September~2022 -- February~2023). Expected returns are generated by
a one-lag AR(1) model: for each stock $j$, the projected return at day $i+1$ is
$\hat{R}_{i+1,j} = \hat{\rho}_j \, R_{i,j}$, where $\hat{\rho}_j$ is the sample
lag-1 autocorrelation. Weights are recomputed daily and the portfolio is evaluated
on four risk/performance metrics: Sharpe ratio, Upside Potential Ratio, maximum
drawdown, and additive volatility contributions.

The principal finding is that the combination of weak AR(1) signals, an unconstrained
optimiser, and a full-sample covariance matrix leads to extreme leverage and to
realised performance that is dramatically worse than a simple equal-weight strategy.
This constitutes both a cautionary tale about unconstrained mean--variance optimisation
and a concrete illustration of look-ahead bias.

% ======================== 3. PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION ========================
\section{Portfolio Construction}

\subsection{Daily Returns}

Each stock's intraday quotes are aggregated to the daily scale by retaining the last
close price of each trading session.  Arithmetic price returns are:
\begin{equation}
  R_{i,j} = \frac{P_{i,j} - P_{i-1,j}}{P_{i-1,j}},
  \qquad 1 \le i \le n-1,\; 1 \le j \le 10.
\end{equation}
This yields a $119 \times 10$ matrix of daily returns. Figure~\ref{fig:returns} shows
the time series for all ten stocks.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig_01_daily_returns}
  \caption{Daily arithmetic price returns for the ten selected stocks.
           Concurrent spikes reflect systematic, market-wide events.}
  \label{fig:returns}
\end{figure}

Returns are approximately centred at zero with occasional large outliers, consistent
with well-documented leptokurtosis in large-cap equity returns. Volatility clustering
is also visible, suggesting GARCH-type dynamics that are not captured by our AR(1) model.

The sample correlation matrix and covariance matrix $\Sigma$ (estimated on the full

All 45 pairwise correlations are positive, with an average of $0.29$.
The strongest dependencies appear between industrially related pairs:

Three pairs involving Sanofi are weakly negative: SAN--MT~($-0.11$),

universes and limits the diversification achievable by any long-only allocation.

\subsection{Lag-1 Autocorrelation of Daily Returns}

For each stock $j$, the lag-1 autocorrelation of daily returns is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\rho}_j
  = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=2}^{n-1}\bigl(R_{i,j}-\bar{R}_j\bigr)
    \bigl(R_{i-1,j}-\bar{R}_j\bigr)}
         {\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\bigl(R_{i,j}-\bar{R}_j\bigr)^{2}}.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering

  \caption{Lag-1 autocorrelation $\hat{\rho}_j$ per stock. Blue bars: slight momentum

small magnitude, these coefficients represent the entire linear predictive signal
available at a one-day lag and motivate their use as expected-return proxies.

\subsection{AR(1) Return Projections}

Under a zero-mean AR(1) assumption, the conditional expectation of the return at day
$i+1$ given day~$i$ is simply:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{R}_{i+1,j} = \hat{\rho}_j \cdot R_{i,j}.
  \label{eq:ar1}
\end{equation}
This is the \emph{best linear one-step-ahead forecast} given a single lag. The method
is parsimonious (one free parameter per stock), requires no future information, and is
well-grounded: $\hat\rho_j$ captures the entirety of the linear dependence between
$R_{i,j}$ and $R_{i+1,j}$ at lag~1. Figure~\ref{fig:ar1} illustrates the projection
for LVMH~(MC, $\hat\rho = -0.138$): the projected series is a strongly attenuated,
sign-reversed version of the previous day's return.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{fig_04_ar1_projection}
  \caption{Realised vs.\ AR(1)-projected daily returns for LVMH~(MC).
           The projection $\hat{R}_{i+1} = -0.138 \cdot R_i$ correctly captures
           the direction of short-term mean reversion but with greatly reduced amplitude.}
  \label{fig:ar1}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Dynamic Markowitz Optimal Weights}

Let $\hat{R}_{i+1} = (\hat{R}_{i+1,1},\ldots,\hat{R}_{i+1,10})'$.
The Markowitz weight vector under the unit-sum constraint is:
\begin{equation}
  \Omega_{i+1}
  = \frac{\Sigma^{-1}\hat{R}_{i+1}}{\mathbf{1}_{10}'\,\Sigma^{-1}\hat{R}_{i+1}}.
  \label{eq:markowitz}
\end{equation}
No short-sale or leverage constraint is imposed; weights can be negative or very large.
$\Sigma$ is the full-sample covariance matrix (see Section~\ref{sec:bias}).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig_05_dynamic_weights}
  \caption{Daily Markowitz weights $\Omega_{i+1}$ over the investment period.
           Extreme excursions (e.g.\ $-7.2$ for SU on day~2) result from near-zero
           denominators $\mathbf{1}'\Sigma^{-1}\hat{R}_{i+1}$ when projected returns
           are small.}
  \label{fig:weights}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:weights} reveals severe time-varying leverage. The time-averaged
weights range from $-3.86$ (AXA, short) to $+3.54$ (LVMH, long), with the sum of
absolute values averaging $\approx 22$---implying effective leverage of $22\times$.
This is the direct consequence of dividing by the scalar
$\mathbf{1}'\Sigma^{-1}\hat{R}_{i+1}$, which is small whenever $\hat{R}_{i+1}$ is
small (i.e.\ whenever the AR(1) forecast is near zero, which is almost always the case
given $|\hat\rho_j| \ll 1$).

\subsection{Realised Portfolio Returns}

The portfolio's daily realised return is $r_{p,i+1} = \Omega_{i+1}' R_{i+1}$ and the
cumulative value (base~100) evolves as $V_{i+1} = V_i \cdot (1 + r_{p,i+1})$.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig_06_portfolio_performance}
  \caption{\emph{Top}: daily realised returns of the Markowitz portfolio; the return
           range $[-507\%,\; +897\%]$ reflects extreme leverage.
           \emph{Bottom}: cumulative performance (base~100). The Markowitz portfolio
           ends at $-1271.82$ versus $+122.89$ for the equal-weight benchmark.}
  \label{fig:performance}
\end{figure}

The strategy suffers a catastrophic realised outcome. The portfolio value ends at
$\mathbf{-1271.82}$ (base~100), while the equal-weight benchmark delivers a modest
gain to $+122.89$. This catastrophe arises because extreme leverage means that even a
small systematic misalignment between projected and realised return directions produces
a loss exceeding the available capital.

\subsection{Methodological Limitation: Look-Ahead Bias}
\label{sec:bias}

The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ in equation~\eqref{eq:markowitz} is estimated on the
\emph{entire} return sample, including data strictly in the future relative to any
trading day~$i$. This is a textbook \textbf{look-ahead bias}: the weights at day~$i+1$
implicitly encode future co-movement structure never available in live deployment.

In the present case the bias acts paradoxically: although $\Sigma$ is full-sample, the
unconstrained optimiser amplifies weak signals into such extreme leverage that the
portfolio still destroys capital in-sample. The bias would inflate apparent performance
if the strategy had a genuine edge; here it cannot rescue a strategy with no real edge.
A rolling-window $\Sigma$ would reduce the bias but, without leverage constraints,
would not fundamentally fix the strategy.

% ======================== 4. RISK AND PERFORMANCE ========================
\section{Risk and Performance Evaluation}

\subsection{Sharpe Ratio}

The Sharpe ratio (risk-free rate $= 0$) is $\mathrm{SR} = \bar{r}_p / \sigma_p$. We
report two variants:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Ex-ante}: using projected portfolio returns
    $\hat{r}_{p,i+1} = \Omega_{i+1}'\hat{R}_{i+1}$.
  \item \textbf{Ex-post}: using realised portfolio returns
    $r_{p,i+1} = \Omega_{i+1}'R_{i+1}$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Sharpe ratio of the Markowitz portfolio (risk-free rate~$= 0$).}
\label{tab:sharpe}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Daily SR} & \textbf{Annualised SR ($\times\sqrt{252}$)} \\
\midrule
Ex-ante  & $0.0604$ & $0.9595$ \\
Ex-post  & $0.0277$ & $0.4394$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The ex-ante annualised Sharpe of $0.96$ looks attractive on paper: the weights are
constructed precisely to maximise the ratio $\Omega'\hat{R} / \sqrt{\Omega'\Sigma\Omega}$,
so by design the projected Sharpe is as high as the AR(1) signal allows. When projected
returns are replaced by realised returns, the Sharpe ratio drops to $0.44$. The ratio
$0.44 / 0.96 \approx 0.46$ measures the fraction of the constructed signal that
survives into realised outcomes---a concrete measure of forecast quality, entirely
driven by the low magnitude of the autocorrelations.

Both ratios are computed on a return series with daily standard deviation $\approx 97\%$
(due to extreme leverage), making the annualised figures poorly interpretable in
conventional portfolio management terms.

\subsection{Upside Potential Ratio}

The Upside Potential Ratio (UPR) measures the payoff asymmetry between gains and losses:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{UPR}
  = \frac{\mathbb{E}\!\left[\max(r_p,\;0)\right]}
         {\sqrt{\mathbb{E}\!\left[\min(r_p,\;0)^{2}\right]}}.
  \label{eq:upr}
\end{equation}
A value above~1 means gains outweigh losses on average in magnitude.

For our portfolio:
\[
  \text{Mean positive return} = 0.212, \quad
  \text{RMS negative return} = 0.655, \quad
  \boxed{\mathrm{UPR} = 0.323.}
\]
The UPR is well below~1: average losses in magnitude ($0.655$ per losing day) are
more than three times larger than average gains ($0.212$ per winning day). This
strongly unfavourable asymmetry arises because leverage amplifies forecast errors
disproportionately: when the AR(1) projection is correct, the gain is small (leverage
is concentrated in the right direction, but $|\hat\rho| \ll 1$ means the expected
return is tiny); when it is wrong---as it often is on large market moves---the loss
is amplified by the same extreme leverage. The result is a left-skewed return
distribution.

\subsection{Maximum Drawdown}

The Maximum Drawdown quantifies the worst peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MDD} = \max_{t_1 < t_2} \frac{V(t_1) - V(t_2)}{V(t_1)}.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{fig_07_drawdown}
  \caption{\emph{Top}: portfolio value with running maximum; peak (14~Oct~2022,
           green) and trough (27~Oct~2022, red) are marked.
           \emph{Bottom}: full drawdown series.}
  \label{fig:drawdown}
\end{figure}

\[
  \mathrm{MDD} = -467.4\%, \quad
  \text{Peak: 14~October~2022}, \quad
  \text{Trough: 27~October~2022.}
\]
The MDD exceeds $-100\%$ because the portfolio goes into deeply negative territory:
the value reaches $-4339.93$ (base~100) from a peak of $+1181.17$, giving a
peak-to-trough loss of $5521 / 1181 \approx 467\%$.

The timing---13 to 27~October~2022---corresponds to the climax of European financial
market stress in Q4~2022, driven by the Bank of England's emergency gilt-market
interventions, peak ECB tightening expectations, and surging energy-price uncertainty.
During this two-week window, European equity returns exhibited unusually large and rapid
reversals. The AR(1) projections, which are based on the previous day's return signal,
systematically pointed in the wrong direction precisely during these high-volatility
reversals. Combined with the extreme leverage positions, even a sequence of moderate
forecast errors was sufficient to plunge the portfolio value into large negative territory.

\subsection{Additive Contribution to Portfolio Volatility}

Portfolio variance decomposes as $\sigma_p^2 = \Omega'\Sigma\Omega =
\sum_j \Omega_j (\Sigma\Omega)_j$. Dividing by $\sigma_p$ gives:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_p = \sum_{j=1}^{10} C_j, \qquad
  C_j = \frac{\Omega_j \cdot (\Sigma\Omega)_j}{\sigma_p},
  \label{eq:vol_decomp}
\end{equation}
where $C_j > 0$ means stock~$j$ amplifies risk and $C_j < 0$ means it acts as a hedge.
The decomposition is evaluated at the time-averaged weight vector $\bar{\Omega}$.

\end{figure}

The portfolio daily volatility is $\sigma_p = 0.0686$ (annualised: $\mathbf{108.9\%}$),
confirming the massive leverage inherent in this unconstrained strategy. By comparison,
the annualised volatility of the individual stocks is approximately $12$--$16\%$.

Risk is highly concentrated: LVMH contributes 27.2\%, AXA 23.3\%, and Sanofi 18.3\%,
together accounting for 68.8\% of total $\sigma_p$. This concentration mirrors their
large absolute mean weights ($|\bar\Omega| \ge 2.77$).

A key observation is that \textbf{all contributions $C_j$ are positive}, including those
of the short positions (AXA, SU, TTE, ORA). This is because $C_j = \Omega_j (\Sigma\Omega)_j / \sigma_p$:
for a short position, $\Omega_j < 0$ and $(\Sigma\Omega)_j < 0$ (since the negative-weight
stock is correlated with the negatively-weighted portfolio direction), so the product is
positive. In other words, every position---long or short---is correlated with the
portfolio in the direction of its own weight, and there is no genuine volatility
cancellation. This is a structural feature of any all-positive-correlation universe
fitted by unconstrained Markowitz: short positions are not hedges; they are simply
opposite-direction bets that still contribute positively to risk.

% ======================== 5. CONCLUSION ========================
\section{Conclusion}

We have constructed and evaluated a daily-rebalanced Markowitz portfolio on ten
CAC~40 stocks, using AR(1) return forecasts as expected-return proxies. The main
findings are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em, label=(\roman*), itemsep=3pt]

  \item \textbf{Cross-asset structure}: all pairwise correlations are positive
    (average~$0.29$), confirming a shared European equity systematic factor. Sanofi
    and Orange provide the lowest correlations with the rest of the universe, validating
    their role as defensive diversifiers.

  \item \textbf{Weak AR(1) signals}: lag-1 autocorrelations lie in $[-0.168,\, 0.123]$.
    Seven stocks show mean reversion; three show slight momentum. The small magnitudes
    of all $\hat\rho_j$ mean that AR(1) projections are essentially noise-dominated.

  \item \textbf{Extreme leverage}: unconstrained Markowitz amplifies near-zero
    projections into daily weight vectors with typical absolute sum of~$\approx 22$.
    This is the direct mechanism by which a theoretically sound framework fails
    catastrophically in practice.

  \item \textbf{Sharpe ratio gap}: ex-ante annualised SR~$= 0.96$ vs.\ ex-post
    annualised SR~$= 0.44$. Only 46\% of the constructed signal survives in realised
    returns. This ratio is a useful measure of forecast quality.

  \item \textbf{UPR of 0.32}: average losses ($-0.655$/event) outweigh gains
    ($+0.212$/event) by a factor of~3, reflecting the strongly left-skewed return
    distribution induced by leveraged AR(1) betting.

  \item \textbf{Maximum drawdown of $-467\%$}: the portfolio ends at $-1272$ (base
    ~100), having briefly reached $+1181$ before crashing. The critical episode
    (14--27~Oct~2022) coincided with peak Q4~2022 European financial market stress.

  \item \textbf{Volatility decomposition}: three stocks (LVMH, AXA, Sanofi) account
    for 68.8\% of portfolio risk. All ten contributions are positive, confirming no
    genuine diversification benefit in an all-positive-correlation universe under
    unconstrained weights.

\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
This exercise powerfully illustrates two independent failure modes that compound. First,
the AR(1) model provides too weak a signal: in efficient markets, $|\hat\rho| \ll 1$,
so any strategy relying solely on lag-1 autocorrelations will struggle to generate
consistent alpha. Second, the unconstrained Markowitz framework amplifies this
near-zero signal into catastrophic leverage, a phenomenon well-documented since
Black and Litterman (1992) and Ledoit and Wolf (2004). Two natural remedies are:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Leverage or position constraints} (e.g.\ $|\Omega_j| \le 0.2$):
    capping positions prevents the amplification of weak signals into extreme bets.
  \item \textbf{Covariance regularisation} (e.g.\ Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage): reducing
    the condition number of $\Sigma$ dampens the blow-up of $\Sigma^{-1}\hat{R}$
    when $\hat{R}$ is small.
\end{itemize}

Both extensions would form natural improvements to the present framework and would
bring the strategy closer to what one would confidently deploy with real capital.
